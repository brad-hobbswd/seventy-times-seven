Seventy Times Seven

Why the Need for Limits Reveals Where Pain Still Has Power

When Peter suggested seven times, he was not being stingy. He was being generous according to human standards. His question reveals a natural desire for control. Limits make pain feel manageable. Numbers give the illusion that suffering has boundaries.

This desire for limits usually forms after repeated disappointment. The first offense is painful. The second is confusing. By the third or fourth, trust begins to erode. Forgiveness then becomes something people do with caution instead of compassion.

In real life, people begin to keep internal records long before they admit it. They remember dates. They recall phrases. They measure how much grace they have already given. This is not because they are cruel. It is because they are trying to protect themselves.

Jesus removed the limit because counting keeps wounds alive. Every number reinforces memory. Every tally mark reopens the injury. Seventy times seven was not a command to forgive more. It was an invitation to stop counting altogether.

Jesus was addressing the root issue. Forgiveness feels impossible when pain still has authority. As long as pain is the reference point, forgiveness will always feel costly. But when Jesus becomes the reference point, forgiveness becomes an act of alignment rather than loss.

Supernaturally, something shifts when counting stops. The mind releases its grip on past moments. The heart begins to live forward instead of backward. Forgiveness ceases to be about what happened and becomes about who Jesus is.

This is not instant. It is a process. But it begins the moment counting ends.
